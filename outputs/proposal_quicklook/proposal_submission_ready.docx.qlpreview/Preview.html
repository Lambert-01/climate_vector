Title: AI-Enabled Community-Based Early Warning Systems Integrating Essential Climate Variables and Aedes Mosquito Surveillance for Dengue Prevention in the African Great Lakes Region

 

Background and rationale

 

Climate variability and environmental change are altering the distribution, abundance, and transmission dynamics of Aedes mosquitoes, the primary vectors of dengue virus (DENV). Changes in temperature, rainfall patterns, humidity, urbanization, vegetation cover, and land-use practices create favorable conditions for vector proliferation and disease emergence. East Africa has experienced increasing reports of dengue outbreaks, highlighting the need for proactive surveillance and climate-informed preparedness strategies.

Rwanda shares extensive borders with Uganda, Tanzania, Burundi, and the DR Congo, facilitating cross-border movement of people, vectors, and pathogens. However, limited information exists on how climate change influences Aedes mosquito ecology and dengue transmission risk across these interconnected ecosystems. Furthermore, genomic surveillance of dengue viruses circulating in mosquito populations remains insufficient, limiting understanding of viral evolution, emergence, and spread.

This project proposes an integrated One Health approach combining climate science, entomology, metatranscriptomic surveillance, mathematical modelling, and community engagement to generate and prospectively validate climate-informed dengue risk signals in selected Rwanda pilot sites. Evidence from neighboring Great Lakes settings will provide exploratory regional context and inform future external validation and scale-up.

 

Aim: To understand how climate variability and environmental change influence Aedes mosquito ecology and dengue virus emergence, and to develop and prospectively validate climate-informed early-action signals and community-based surveillance workflows in selected Rwanda pilot sites, with exploratory regional context from neighboring Great Lakes settings.

Research question

RQ1. What are the spatial and temporal patterns of Aedes mosquito populations in Rwanda's border regions?

RQ2. How do essential climate variables affect mosquito abundance and breeding habitats?

RQ3. What dengue virus strains are circulating within Aedes mosquito populations in the study areas?

RQ4. To what extent can essential climate variables, standardized entomological observations, and genomic evidence generate calibrated dengue risk signals with useful operational lead time?

RQ5. How effective are community-based mosquito reporting systems in strengthening dengue surveillance?

RQ6. How can regularly updated risk intelligence and human-reviewed signals support timely public health preparedness and targeted surveillance?

Methodology

 

Objective 1. Adult and larvae Aedes mosquitoes will be collected across selected study sites using a combination of entomological surveillance methods, including BG-sentinel traps, ovitraps, larval surveys, and aspirator collections. These approaches will enable the capture of mosquitoes at different life stages and provide comprehensive data on vector abundance, diversity, and distribution. Concurrently, climate and environmental data will be collected to assess factors influencing mosquito ecology and dengue transmission risk. Essential climate variables like temperature, rainfall, relative humidity, vegetation indices NDVI, land surface temperature, land-use and land-cover changes, as well as elevation and hydrological characteristics. These data will be obtained from national meteorological databases, satellite remote sensing platforms such as NASA and Copernicus, and local environmental monitoring systems. Data analysis will involve GIS-based mapping to characterize the spatial distribution of Aedes mosquito populations, time-series analyses to examine seasonal and temporal trends, and climate with vector association modeling to quantify relationships between environmental variables and mosquito abundance. Spatial statistical techniques will be applied to identify and characterize dengue transmission hotspots and areas at risk due to climate and environmental change.

 

Objective 2. Collected Aedes mosquitoes will be pooled (100 mosquitoes per pool) according to species, geographical location, and collection period to facilitate comprehensive viral surveillance and comparative analyses across ecological settings. Total RNA will be extracted from the mosquito pools and using metatranscriptomic sequencing under MinION Nanopore sequencing. This approach will enable the characterization of the entire mosquito-associated virome, including the detection of known and potentially novel emerging viruses. Bioinformatics analyses will be conducted to identify and classify detected viruses, determine circulating dengue virus serotypes, and reconstruct viral genomes for phylogenetic investigations. Further analyses will explore patterns of viral evolution, genetic diversity, and transmission dynamics, as well as identify genomic markers associated with viral emergence, adaptation, transmission, and outbreak potential. The expected outputs is comprehensive profiles of Aedes mosquito-associated viromes, and the establishment of a regional dengue genomic database to support ongoing arbovirus surveillance and evidence-based public health interventions.

 

Objective 3. Develop and prospectively validate the DengueEW-GL proof-of-concept platform (https://climate-vector-web.vercel.app/) by integrating essential climate variables, standardized Aedes surveillance, dengue virus genomic data, population context, and human-mobility data where governed access is available. Statistical and machine-learning approaches, including generalized additive and mixed-effects models, distributed-lag models, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, and Bayesian spatiotemporal models, will be evaluated only after valid outcomes and sufficient coverage exist. The platform will provide regularly updated weekly or monthly climate-vector risk intelligence, uncertainty, interactive maps, and human-reviewed early-action signals linked to field verification and authorized response. High-resolution products will focus on selected Rwanda pilot sites; neighboring Great Lakes settings will be shown as exploratory context for future external validation.

 

Objective 4. Establish a community-based surveillance component through focus group discussions, photovoice, and co-design with community health workers, schools, local leaders, farmers, and environmental health officers. Mobile reporting will capture standing water, containers, potential breeding sites, environmental conditions, mosquito nuisance observations, photographs, coordinates, consent, and follow-up. Community members will not be expected to identify mosquito species or quantify abundance. Trained entomology teams will verify mosquito identity and abundance using standardized field methods. Participation, data quality, validation time, completed actions, knowledge, and source-reduction practices will be evaluated throughout the pilot.

1. What is the specific gap or problem addressed by your innovation?

Describe the climate-driven health problem that your innovation addresses, providing details on the specific context you propose working in. How does it support local actors in adapting and building resilience to the health impacts of climate change?

Climate change is increasing the risk of dengue and other Aedes mosquito-borne diseases across East Africa. Rising temperatures, changing rainfall patterns, urbanization, flooding, and environmental degradation are creating favorable conditions for Aedes mosquito breeding in Rwanda and its bordering countries such as Uganda, Tanzania, Burundi, and the DR Congo.

Despite increasing reports of dengue in the African Great Lakes region, surveillance remains fragmented and largely reactive. Essential climate variables, standardized entomological observations, genomic evidence, and community environmental reports are rarely integrated into a governed decision-support workflow that can be prospectively evaluated. Evidence on climate-Aedes relationships and dengue virus emergence in Rwanda and neighboring ecosystems remains limited, while local actors lack timely mechanisms to report potential breeding conditions and receive verified follow-up.

Our innovation addresses this gap through DengueEW-GL, an AI-enabled, climate-informed early warning and community surveillance proof of concept. It will integrate climate intelligence, prospective Aedes surveillance, dengue genomic data, and verified community reports to generate regularly updated risk intelligence and human-reviewed early-action signals for targeted surveillance and preparedness.

The proposed study will support local adaptation and resilience by empowering communities, community health workers, schools, local leaders, and environmental health officers to actively participate in dengue control and prevention. Through mobile-based reporting tools, community co-design processes, FGD, and photovoice methods, local actors will identify risks, report mosquito breeding sites, and develop context-specific preparedness solutions. By combining advanced analytics with local knowledge, the innovation strengthens the capacity of health systems and communities to anticipate, prepare for, and respond to climate-sensitive disease threats.

The primary population of focus includes climate-vulnerable communities living in Rwanda’s dengue-prone and border regions, particularly communities located near the borders with Uganda, Tanzania, Burundi, and the DR Congo. These populations are disproportionately vulnerable because climate change is increasing Aedes mosquito breeding habitats through essential climate variables. CHWs, teacher at schools, local leaders, and environmental health officers will receive training to support dengue surveillance, and local response actions.

By providing accessible early warnings, strengthening local surveillance capacity, and promoting community-led vector control measures, the innovation will help communities anticipate climate-related dengue risks, reduce Aedes mosquito breeding sites, improve preparedness, and strengthen resilience to the growing health impacts of climate change.

Essential climate variables and environmental data will be collected from meteorological and satellite sources. AEDES mosquito samples will undergo metatranscriptomic sequencing to identify circulating dengue viruses and characterize viral diversity later on spatial analyses will be conducted to identify mosquito hotspots and climate-risk patterns.

Milestones: Baseline mosquito distribution maps will be developed, identify essential climate variables and environmental risk factors associated with Aedes mosquitoes, generate Aedes mosquito virome profile, Regional Aedes mosquito surveillance database will be established.

Phase 3: AI Model Development and Early Warning System Deployment (Months 10-20)

The team will integrate essential climate variables, standardized entomological data, genomic results, and verified community reports into the pre-developed DengueEW-GL platform (https://climate-vector-web.vercel.app/). Candidate statistical and machine-learning models will be tested only when outcome, effort, spatial, and temporal requirements are met. The platform will present uncertainty, interactive maps, and human-reviewed signals linked to field verification and response workflows.

Milestones: the community reporting workflow will be field-tested; prospective Aedes and genomic records will be quality-controlled; candidate models will be evaluated with predefined validation metrics; and authorized health actors will test the review, verification, assignment, acknowledgment, and follow-up workflow.

Phase 4: Community co-design, pilot testing, and refinement (Months 14-22)

Community members such as CHW, school teachers, local leaders, farmers in the cooperatives, and environmental health officers will participate in training and pilot implementation. Participant’s feedback will be collected to improve platform functionality, usability, reporting mechanisms, and communication strategies.

Milestones

Develop co design preparedness solutions with community, implementation the pilot system, assess the user experience and system performance, deploy the refined version of the system

Phase 5: Dissemination, knowledge Translation, and scale-up planning (Months 18-24)

The project will evaluate technical reliability, model calibration and discrimination, community participation, operational lead time, false-alert burden, response completion, and adoption of preventive practices. Findings will be shared through policy briefs, technical reports, peer-reviewed outputs, and regional learning activities. A sustainability and scale-up roadmap will define the evidence and partnerships required for expansion.

Milestones: Complete the evaluation of proof-of-concept, publication of peer review article, policy briefs and technical reports to the funder, develop the scale-up and sustainability plan, and regional dissemination workshop will be conducted.

Continuous monitoring, stakeholder feedback, and participatory evaluation will refine the innovation. Key deliverables will include an operational DengueEW-GL pilot platform; standardized Aedes, climate, genomic, and community workflows; regularly updated pilot-site risk maps with uncertainty; a dengue genomic registry; community sentinel networks; human-reviewed early-action and response workflows; prospective model-validation results; and recommendations for climate-resilient dengue surveillance and control.

Successful implementation assumes continued collaboration among government, research, and community partners; availability of climate and environmental data; community willingness to participate in surveillance activities; adequate mobile connectivity for digital reporting; and sustained institutional commitment to integrating the early warning system into routine public health practice. If these conditions are met, the project will provide a scalable model for climate-resilient surveillance and prevention of dengue and other climate-sensitive vector-borne diseases in Rwanda and the wider region.

 

5. What is the Proof of Concept you are trying to establish and what specific results will determine if the innovation has successfully demonstrated this within the timeframe of the grant?

Describe the specific Proof of Concept you are trying to establish and provide 4-6 key objectives intended to demonstrate its successful achievement. These objectives should be measurable outcome indicators that you will be able to monitor and assess throughout the pilot period.

 

The proof of concept will test whether an AI-enabled, climate-informed, community-based system can generate useful and prospectively validated dengue risk signals by integrating climate and environmental data, standardized Aedes surveillance, dengue virus genomic evidence, and verified community reports. It will also assess technical, operational, ethical, and financial feasibility in selected Rwanda pilot sites.

The central hypothesis is that governed integration of these data streams will provide more timely, calibrated, and actionable risk intelligence than fragmented surveillance, enabling local actors to target verification and preparedness earlier. Predictive and operational performance will be measured rather than assumed.

The PoC will be considered successfully demonstrated if the following measurable objectives are achieved during the 24-month implementation period:

Objective 1, establish an operational climate-informed dengue early warning system.

A functional platform integrating climate, environmental, entomological, genomic, and verified community data will operate in selected pilot districts. Regularly updated dashboards, uncertainty-labelled maps, and human-reviewed signals will be accessible to authorized public health stakeholders.

Objective 2, demonstrate accurate climate-sensitive dengue risk prediction.

Candidate models will generate monthly risk estimates and priority maps when minimum evidence requirements are met. Performance will be evaluated prospectively using calibration, sensitivity, specificity, precision, recall, ROC-AUC, PR-AUC, Brier score, operational lead time, geographic and temporal holdouts, and false-alert burden. Thresholds will be agreed with health authorities; no single accuracy percentage will determine success.

Objective 3, strengthen climate-informed vector and genomic surveillance.

Aedes mosquito distribution, abundance, and climate-risk relationships are mapped across study sites. Identify dengue viruses circulating in mosquito populations and genetically characterized through metatranscriptomic sequencing. Generate a regional dengue genomic surveillance database

Objective 4, demonstrate effective community participation in dengue surveillance and preparedness.

Community sentinel networks will report standing water, containers, potential breeding sites, environmental conditions, mosquito nuisance observations, photographs, and locations. Trained entomology teams will verify mosquito identity and abundance. At least 70% of trained participants will be targeted for regular reporting, while participation quality, consent, validation, and follow-up will also be monitored.

Objective 5, improve preparedness and response capacity.

Public health authorities and local stakeholders will use risk forecasts and alerts to guide vector-control planning and outbreak preparedness activities. Participating of communities will increased knowledge of dengue prevention and adoption of recommended source-reduction and vector-control practices.

Objective 6, demonstrate feasibility and scalability of the innovation.

End users report high levels of usability, acceptability, and trust in the platform. Therefore, a sustainability and scale-up strategy will be developed with RBC-MoH, to integrate into routine surveillance systems and expansion to additional districts and climate-sensitive vector-borne diseases.

Achievement of these objectives will provide robust evidence that the innovation can strengthen climate resilience, improve dengue preparedness, and serve as a scalable model for climate-informed disease surveillance and early warning systems in countries located in African Great Lakes Region

 

6. What is your monitoring and evaluation plan?

Describe your monitoring and evaluation plan, including your approach to measurement, as well as the (quantitative and qualitative) measurement tool(s) and data analysis methods you will use to track and evaluate the impact of your intervention. Are there limitations to your approach?

If your innovation intends to deliver certain results cheaper/quicker/of better quality, how will you assess your innovation’s performance in these areas as compared to existing approaches?

 

The project will implement a robust mixed-methods Monitoring, Evaluation, and Learning (MEL) framework to assess the effectiveness, accuracy, usability, and climate adaptation value of the AI-enabled dengue early warning system. The MEL framework will measure whether integrating essential climate variables, entomological data, genomic data, and community reporting strengthens dengue preparedness, improves outbreak prediction, and enhances community resilience to climate-sensitive health risks.

A baseline, midline, and endline evaluation will be conducted over the 24-month period. The platform will provide regularly updated operational metrics to support adaptive learning, model review, and system refinement.

Quantitative measurement methods will include data collected through the digital early warning platform, entomological data, genomic data, climate databases, and community reporting systems. Key indicators will include 1) number of operational surveillance sites established, 2) number of Aedes mosquitoes collected, identified, and analysed, 3) number of dengue-positive mosquito pools and viral genomes characterized, 4) number of community data submitted through the platform, 5) number of trained community participants and stakeholders, 6) frequency of dashboard use, risk map access, and alert utilization, 7) timeliness and accuracy of outbreak forecasts, 8) changes in community knowledge and adoption of dengue prevention practices.

 

Data analysis will include descriptive statistics, time-series analysis, GIS-based hotspot mapping, regression modelling, and ML performance assessment. Predictive models will be evaluated using sensitivity, specificity, precision, recall, F1-score, and Area Under the Curve (AUC) metrics. Spatial and temporal analyses will assess how climate variables influence mosquito abundance and dengue risk.

Qualitative evaluation methods will assess user experiences, acceptability, community ownership, and decision-making value. FGDs, photovoice exercises, KIIs, and stakeholder workshops will be conducted with CHWs, local leaders, teachers, environmental health officers, and public health decision-makers. Thematic analysis will be used to identify lessons learned, barriers to adoption, and opportunities for improvement.

The project will also assess whether the innovation delivers results faster, better, and more efficiently than existing surveillance approaches. Current dengue surveillance systems are largely reactive and depend on human case detection at health centers of national refence laboratory. Comparison indicators will include 1) time between risk detection and potential outbreak occurrence, 2) ability to identify hotspots before reported human cases emerge, 3) increased reporting rates and community participation, 4) improved targeting of vector-control interventions, 5) enhanced preparedness actions triggered by forecasts and alerts, 6) cost and resource savings resulting from risk-based interventions compared with broad reactive responses.

To support this comparison, platform outputs will be evaluated against routine surveillance data and historical outbreak patterns where available. Public health stakeholders will also assess the usefulness of forecasts in planning and decision-making.

Potential limitations include incomplete historical dengue data, variability in climate conditions, underreporting from communities, uneven mobile phone access, and digital literacy challenges. Forecast accuracy may also be affected by changing environmental and epidemiological conditions. To address these limitations, the project will use multiple complementary data sources, regular data-quality assessments, user training, continuous model recalibration, and iterative system improvements informed by stakeholder feedback.

This monitoring and evaluation approach will provide rigorous evidence on the feasibility, effectiveness, and scalability of the innovation while supporting continuous learning and adaptation throughout implementation.

 

7. How is this proposed innovation designed so as to consider issues of sustainability at this early Proof of Concept stage?

Consider current and future needs related to sustaining quality impact and financially sustaining operations. Describe any barriers to sustainability and how your innovation will seek to address them.

 

Sustainability has been embedded into the innovation from the outset to ensure that the benefits continue beyond the Proof-of-Concept period and can be integrated into existing health and climate adaptation systems. The project is designed to build sustainable technical, institutional, community, and financial foundations for long-term climate-sensitive disease surveillance and response.

Institutional sustainability will be achieved by co-developing the innovation with key national stakeholders, including the RBC, MoH, REMA, RSA, RAB, and the UR. By aligning the platform with existing national surveillance, climate monitoring, and public health systems, the project will create a pathway for integration into routine government operations rather than establishing a parallel system.

Community sustainability is central to the innovation design, community groups will participate as active partners in surveillance, reporting, and preparedness activities. Through training, co-design workshops, will develop ownership of the intervention and strengthen local capacity to monitor and respond to climate-sensitive disease risks. This community-driven model supports long-term use and maintenance of surveillance activities beyond the grant period is over.

Technical sustainability will be supported through the use of scalable digital infrastructure and automated data integration processes. The platform will be designed to incorporate routinely essential climate variables, environmental and entomological data, reducing dependence on intensive manual data collection. The modular design will also allow future expansion to additional districts, neighboring countries, and other climate-sensitive vector-borne diseases like Rift Valley Fever, and Chikukunya

Financial sustainability will be addressed through early engagement with government agencies like RBC, REMA, RAB and development partners to identify opportunities for long-term adoption and co-financing. By demonstrating the value of early outbreak detection and targeted interventions particularly for arbovirus diseases, the project aims to show cost savings compared to expensive reactive outbreak responses. Evidence generated during the Proof-of-Concept phase will support future investment, government ownership, and integration into NHIC.

Key sustainability barriers include limited long-term financing, variations in digital connectivity, staff turnover, and ongoing maintenance requirements for surveillance systems. These risks will be mitigated through capacity building, institutional partnerships, use of low-cost and accessible technologies, integration into existing systems hosted by MoH known as NHIC, and development of a sustainability and scale-up roadmap during the project period.

Proof-of-Concept is designed not only to demonstrate technical effectiveness but also to establish a sustainable model that can be adopted, financed, and scaled by national institutions and communities. This approach will ensure continued impact in strengthening climate resilience, dengue preparedness, and public health adaptation to climate change in Rwanda and across the region.
